Social Engineering Attacks

  • Social Engineering attack is one kind of cyber attacks, it is art of manipulating the individuals to share confidential information or performing some actions that compromise the security.
  • Social engineering relies on psychological manipulation rather than exploiting software vulnerabilities.

Common Types of Social Engineering Attacks:

  1. Phishing:

    • Description: Attackers send fraudulent emails or messages that appear to come from a trusted source, like a bank or a company, to trick recipients into providing sensitive information or clicking on malicious links.
    • Example: An email pretending (act like ) to be from a bank asks you to verify your account details by clicking a link that leads to a fake login page.

  2. Spear Phishing

    • Description: Attacker target specific individuals or specific group to steal sensitive information.
    • Example: An email that appears to come from a colleague, using information from social media, asking for sensitive company information.

  3. Whale phishing

    • Description: Attacker speciallly target wealthy, higher level, powerfull people…
      Ex: CEO, CFO etc…

  4. Pretexting

    • Description: Attackers create a fabricated scenario (pretext) to obtain information from the target. They often impersonate someone in a position of authority or a trusted entity.
    • Example: A caller pretending to be an IT support technician asks for login credentials to resolve a supposed technical issue.

  5. Baiting

    • Description: Attackers use promises of free items or benefits to lure victims into downloading malicious software or providing sensitive information.
    • Example: A fake website offers free software or a prize, but downloading the file infects your computer with malware.

  6. Tailgating (or Piggybacking)

    • Description: An attacker gains physical access to a secure area by following someone with legitimate access, often by pretending to be an authorized employee or visitor.
    • Example: An attacker asks someone to hold the door open to a secure building while they enter behind them.

  7. Vishing (Voice Phishing)

    • Description: Attackers use phone calls to impersonate legitimate entities and trick victims into revealing sensitive information.
    • Example: A caller claims to be from your bank and asks for personal information to “verify your identity.”

  8. Smishing (SMS Phishing)

    • Description: Attackers send fraudulent text messages to trick recipients into providing personal information or clicking on malicious links.
    • Example: A text message that appears to be from a delivery service, asking you to click a link to track a package, which leads to a phishing site.

  9. Quishing

    • Description: Quishing (a combination of “QR code” and “phishing”) involves the use of malicious QR codes to trick users into visiting fraudulent websites or downloading malicious software. Attackers create and distribute QR codes that, when scanned, lead to phishing sites or initiate harmful actions.

    • Example: An attacker places a QR code in a public place or sends it via email, promising something of value (like a discount or free prize). Scanning the QR code redirects users to a fake website that looks legitimate, where they may be prompted to enter personal information or download malware.

  10. Impersonation

    • Description: Attackers pose as someone you know or a trusted authority to gain access to confidential information or systems.
    • Example: An attacker pretends to be a company executive to request sensitive data from an employee.

How to Protect Against Social Engineering Attacks:

  • Be skeptical (సందేహంగా ఉండండి): Question unsolicited requests, even if they seem to come from trusted sources.
  • Verify information: Double-check email addresses, phone numbers, and websites before responding.
  • Avoid sharing personal information: Be cautious about sharing sensitive data online or over the phone.
  • Use strong passwords: Create complex and unique passwords for all of your accounts.
  • Use MFA: To provide extra layer of security.
  • Use filters: Implement anti phishing and anti spam filter and other policies to detect suspicious/spam emails.
  • Be aware of phishing scams: Watch out for emails or messages that ask for personal information or contain suspicious links.
  • Be Cautious with Emails and Links: Avoid clicking on suspicious links or opening unknown attachments.
  • Trust your instincts: If something feels off, it probably is. Don’t hesitate to reach out to a trusted source for advice.

Steps to follow if user clicks on url/attachment from suspicious email:

By taking below steps, you can minimize the potential impact of a suspicious email and enhance your overall security posture to prevent future incidents.

1. Disconnect from the Internet

  • Immediate Action: Disconnect the affected device from the internet to prevent any potential malware from communicating with remote servers or spreading further.

2. Notify IT or Security Team

  • Report the Incident: Inform your IT department or cybersecurity team about the suspicious email and any actions taken. Provide them with details about the email, the attachment, and what was clicked or opened.

3. Do Not Interact Further

  • Avoid Additional Actions: Refrain from clicking on any additional links or opening other attachments from the same email.

4. Run a Security Scan

  • Use Antivirus/Anti-Malware Software: Run a full system scan with your antivirus or anti-malware software to detect and remove any threats. If any threats detected, immediately block those IOCs like URL, domain, IP, Hash on security solutions.

5. Change Passwords

  • Update Credentials: Change passwords for any accounts that may have been compromised or accessed. Use a different, secure device to perform this action.

6. Monitor for Unusual Activity

  • Watch for Signs of Compromise: Check for any unusual or unauthorized activities in your accounts, such as unfamiliar transactions or changes.

7. Update Software

  • Apply Patches and Updates: Ensure that your operating system and all software are up to date with the latest security patches.

8. Restore from Backup (if necessary)

  • Check Backups: If malware or data corruption is detected, consider restoring affected files or systems from clean backups. Ensure backups are free of malware before restoring.

9. Educate Yourself and Others

  • Learn from the Incident: Understand how the phishing attempt was conducted and educate yourself and your colleagues to avoid similar attacks in the future.

10. Review Security Measures

  • Enhance Security Practices: Assess and improve security measures, such as email filtering, to prevent similar incidents. Consider implementing additional security training and awareness programs.

11. Document the Incident

  • Keep Records: Document the incident details, actions taken, and any findings. This can help in understanding the attack vector and improving future responses.
